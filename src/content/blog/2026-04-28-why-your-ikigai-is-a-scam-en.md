## The Great Lie of the Ikigai Diagram

Let's get one thing straight: the Ikigai diagram flooding your LinkedIn feed is a purely Western invention. It has nothing to do with ancient Japanese wisdom. A blogger named Marc Winn popularized it in 2014, merging a Venn diagram on the « purpose of life » by Spanish astrologer Andrés Zuzunaga (2011) with the Japanese concept of Ikigai. The real Ikigai, as neuroscientist Ken Mogi explains in his book *« The Little Book of Ikigai »*, is much simpler and more humble. It's about the small things that make you want to get up in the morning: the taste of coffee, a good conversation, the sun on your face. It's a philosophy of daily life, not a grand career plan.

The Western version imposes an obsession with monetization and performance onto a concept that, originally, had nothing to do with them. This confusion creates immense pressure: you must not only love what you do but also be good at it, and on top of that, the world must need it and be willing to pay you. It's a quest for a perfect formula that more often leads to paralysis than to action.

## Julia de Funès: No, Work Isn't Here to Make You Happy

The French philosopher Julia de Funès goes further, stating that « happiness in the workplace is an absurdity ». This deliberately provocative sentence denounces the corporate trend of managing your well-being with Chief Happiness Officers, foosball tables, and « team-building » seminars. For her, these gimmicks hide the real problem and infantilize employees. Meaning at work doesn't come from a fun environment, but from two much deeper things: **direction** and **significance**.

Direction is knowing where you are going, understanding your company's overall vision and your place in that project. Significance is perceiving the concrete utility of your daily tasks. Without these two elements, even the best-paying job feels hollow. Julia de Funès reminds us that meaning is an individual construction. It cannot be decreed. It is built through action, autonomy, and sometimes, the courage to say no to absurd processes. Waiting for your manager to « give » you meaning is an illusion. If you feel like you're experiencing a [quiet burnout at work](/blog/soft-suffering-at-work-the-silent-burnout-en), it might be the signal that you're missing one of these components.

## The Real Ailment of Your Generation: Bore-out and Brown-out

While everyone is talking about burn-out, two other, quieter afflictions are plaguing young professionals: bore-out and brown-out. Bore-out, or exhaustion from boredom, affects about 6% of employees in France, according to Professor Olivier Meier in HBR France. It's what happens when your job is so devoid of challenges and stimulation that your energy completely drains. You're tired not from doing too much, but from doing nothing that matters.

Brown-out, on the other hand, is the loss of meaning. You have work, you might even be busy, but your tasks seem so pointless, so disconnected from any real purpose, that you end up mentally disengaging. A 2024 study in France by Ignition Program is alarming: 47% of employees are in a state of mental disengagement. That's nearly one in two. These syndromes explain the feeling of emptiness that the Ikigai diagram claims to solve. The problem isn't that you lack passion, but that your work environment nourishes neither your intelligence nor your need to be useful.

## 3 Concrete Steps to Build Your Path (Without the Post-it Notes)

So, what's the plan? If the diagram is a dead end, where do you start? Forget the Post-it notes and planning yourself into a headache. Clarity comes from action, not endless reflection.

### 1. Identify Your "Vital Impulses"
Instead of listing your « skills » or « passions », look for what naturally sets you in motion. What topics can you read about for hours? What problems do you enjoy solving, even for free? Your vital impulses are more reliable indicators than the skills on your resume. They point toward what intrinsically nourishes you, beyond social or financial recognition.

### 2. Confront Your Ideas with Reality
Think you want to go freelance, start a newsletter, or learn to code? Don't wait for the perfect plan. Start experimenting. Take on a micro-project, talk to three people already in that field, take a one-hour online course. The goal is to gather real-world data, not to fantasize about an ideal career change. Every small experiment is a test that brings you closer to or further from a path, at a minimal cost.

### 3. Demand (or Create) Autonomy
Meaning comes from impact. And impact comes from autonomy. Whether in your current job or a future project, look for spaces where you can take initiative and own a project from start to finish. If your work environment is too rigid, start building a side project. Autonomy is a muscle. The more you exercise it, the more capable you feel of directing your own trajectory.

## Conclusion

Stop looking for a magic solution in a diagram. The Westernized Ikigai is a tempting marketing gimmick, but it's a dead end. It makes you believe there's a perfect job out there waiting to be discovered. The reality is simpler and more demanding: meaning isn't found, it's built. It emerges from action, from trials, from errors, and from an honest confrontation with what truly drives you, beyond the ambient noise.
